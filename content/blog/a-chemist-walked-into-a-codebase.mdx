## The Weekend That Changed Things

In late January I got a new laptop from work. A few days later, I got fired.

That's a different story. But you should know the emotional backdrop for this one.

[Gian Pierre](https://www.gianpierre.co.uk/) came to visit that same weekend — one of those timing coincidences that turns out to mean something. We've been friends for eight years. He's a chemist, lives in London, earns £35K a year. He also brought his Windows laptop, which was so catastrophically slow I couldn't even get Gmail to load on it. Not the email. The login screen.

I gave him my old MacBook Pro. Just handed it over. What was I going to do with two laptops when I'd just been sacked?

I didn't know at the time that I was handing him the on-ramp to a completely different life.

## Who Is Gian Pierre?

He's not a developer. He's never had a software engineering job. His background is molecular structures and lab work. When he came to visit, he was in the middle of a Figma UX course — GlassBank, the inclusive banking app he'd later ship, started as that class project.

He wanted to be a UX designer, not a developer. That matters. He wasn't trying to learn to code. He was trying to solve a problem.

Meanwhile, I was busy perfecting something I'd been calling the BAZDMEG method — obsessing over my own system, convinced I was onto something. Gian Pierre had read my other post, [Think Slowly, Ship Fast](/blog/think-slowly-ship-fast), and apparently taken it far more seriously than most people do.

A few weeks later, he sent me a chat app he'd built.

No context. No preamble. Just a screen recording.

I stared at it for a full minute before responding.

*HOW DID YOU DO THAT??*

## The Wrong Instinct

When I finally understood what he'd done, my first reaction was embarrassment. Not at him — at myself. Because I'd been so focused on my own clever system that I missed the obvious thing he'd figured out by instinct.

If you're a developer, you probably start a project like this: pick a framework, set up files and folders, build from the inside out — components, routes, state management. You build the scaffolding first, then fill it with meaning.

Gian Pierre couldn't do that. He didn't have the mental model. When you've spent your career thinking about chemical reactions, you don't think in terms of React components. You think in terms of what a system *does*.

He knew the coaching app needed to book sessions, track frameworks, have an AI that remembers context, manage client progress. These aren't technical details. They're capabilities. Business logic written in plain English.

So instead of building an app that calls APIs, he built the capabilities first — small, typed, testable units of business logic — and wrapped a UI around them afterward.

In MCP terms: he built the tools before he built the interface.

## What "Think Slowly, Ship Fast" Actually Looks Like

This is the part I want to be precise about, because it's not magic.

Gian Pierre had read the Hourglass Model post. He understood the core idea: heavy specs at the top, disposable UI in the middle, bulletproof business logic at the bottom. Most people read that and nod. He actually did it.

He built what we call **MCP (Model Context Protocol) tools** — small functions with clear typed inputs, outputs, and plain-language descriptions of what they do. Each tool was a self-contained unit of business logic. Think of them as virtual logic lego blocks: reusable, testable, and something an AI agent can actually *use* while it's planning.

Then he designed the whole app from those MCPs outward.

When Claude Code planned the implementation, it didn't have to guess what the backend would return. It could call the tools. It could see the exact responses before writing a single line of frontend code. And because Gian Pierre had designed at the MCP level first, the plan was clear enough that Gemini Flash could understand and implement it flawlessly.

<Callout type="info">
When an AI agent tests the tools it depends on during the planning phase, you get three things: faster development, more tests, and fewer bugs. The agent naturally tests everything as it explores.
</Callout>

The result? 100% code coverage on the coaching platform. Not because anyone mandated it. Because the AI naturally wrote tests to understand what the tools did before building on top of them.

## What He Actually Built

He didn't build one app. He built three. Check them yourself at [gianpierre.co.uk](https://www.gianpierre.co.uk/).

**The Coaching App** — an evidence-based coaching platform with 24 structured lessons across 6 modules, 7 coaching frameworks, 6 interactive exercise types, a life satisfaction radar chart measuring 8 dimensions, a streak and badge gamification system, and a reflective journal. Built with Next.js 16, Drizzle ORM, and NextAuth. Full competitive audit of BetterUp, Marlee, Noom, and four others. Five user personas with willingness-to-pay analysis. Deployed to production.

**HealthBridge** — an AI symptom triage assistant powered by Google Gemini 2.5 Flash. A full RAG pipeline with cosine similarity search and text-embedding-004. Three specialized tools with Zod validation schemas. Multi-step reasoning that chains up to 3 tool calls per turn. Emergency overrides that bypass all reasoning for chest pain and stroke signs. Streaming token-by-token UI. This is not a tutorial project. This is production-grade AI engineering.

**GlassBank** — the app that started as a Figma class project. An inclusive mobile banking app with WCAG 2.1 AA accessibility, a glassmorphism design system, dual-interface modes (Standard and Simplified), and a competitive analysis of 6 banks. [Live on Vercel](https://glassbank-app.vercel.app/). The UI is beautiful because it was written by a highly capable model working from a well-designed spec. The business logic underneath is typed and tested.

A chemist built all of this. In a few evenings. After his day job. On a MacBook that used to be mine.

## The £90,000 Question

Let's do the math. Not to be cute — to be honest about what the software industry actually costs.

A traditional team to build Gian Pierre's three projects:

| Role | Duration | Cost |
|------|----------|------|
| Senior Full-Stack Developer | 6 weeks | £11,250 |
| AI/ML Engineer | 4 weeks | £8,500 |
| UX Designer | 4 weeks | £5,500 |
| Frontend Developer | 4 weeks | £6,000 |
| Product Manager | 10 weeks | £17,500 |
| Scrum Master | 10 weeks | £13,750 |
| **Total** | | **£62,500 - £120,000** |

Gian Pierre did it in roughly 60-80 hours. Midpoint cost to hire a team: £90,000. His effective rate: **£1,286 per hour**.

For context, a UK senior developer earns £40-75/hour. A McKinsey consultant bills £300-500/hour. Gian Pierre, the chemist, produced £1,286/hour of market value.

But here's the part that should make you uncomfortable. Look at that team table again. The Product Manager and the Scrum Master together cost **£31,250**. Half the budget. Their entire job is translating between the person who understands the problem and the people who can build it.

Gian Pierre IS the person who understands the problem. The AI IS the builder. The translation layer — the most expensive line item — simply didn't need to exist.

Also: I got fired while writing about AI making jobs obsolete. I'm choosing to call that irony. Gian Pierre chose to call it an opportunity.

## The Bullshit Jobs Problem

In 2018, the anthropologist David Graeber published [*Bullshit Jobs*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bullshit_Jobs). His thesis: a huge portion of the modern economy consists of jobs that even the people doing them secretly believe are pointless. He identified five types.

Every single one exists in a typical software team.

**Flunkies** — people whose job is to make someone else feel important. In software: the senior engineer whose primary output is attending leadership meetings and nodding. Gian Pierre had no org chart to perform for.

**Goons** — roles that exist only because competitors have them. An arms race of pointlessness. In software: adopting Kubernetes because your competitor did. Gian Pierre picked Next.js because it solved his problem, not because it was on a tech radar published by a consultancy whose business model depends on you feeling behind.

**Duct Tapers** — people hired to fix problems that shouldn't exist. In software: the platform engineer who spends their career managing YAML files for a deployment pipeline that a `git push` to Vercel would have replaced. MCP-first means the abstraction was right from the start, so nobody needed to tape over it.

**Box Tickers** — people who create the appearance of productivity without the substance. Sprint retrospectives that change nothing. Velocity charts that are gamed. JIRA ticket grooming sessions that exist because someone once read the Scrum Guide and confused ritual with progress. Gian Pierre's process: describe capability, build, ship. No ceremonies were harmed in the making of his software.

**Taskmasters** — people whose job is to create work for others. The project manager who translates business requirements into JIRA stories so developers can translate JIRA stories back into... business requirements, but in TypeScript. Gian Pierre collapsed this entire game of telephone into one step: tell the AI what the system should do.

<Callout type="warning">
Graeber cited a YouGov poll: 37% of British workers believe their own job makes no meaningful contribution to the world. Apply that to a 6-person dev team. That's 2.2 people. At £65K average salary, that's **£143,000 per year** spent on work that even the workers think is pointless. Per team.
</Callout>

## Human Nature: The Real Bug

Here's the part nobody wants to talk about.

We didn't build this mountain of ceremony because we're stupid. We built it because we're *human*. And humans have some deeply inconvenient firmware.

**We confuse complexity with competence.** A developer who sets up Kubernetes for a three-person startup isn't solving a scaling problem. They're performing sophistication. The same instinct that made medieval monks illuminate manuscripts with gold leaf makes modern engineers add a service mesh to a TODO app. It feels like work. It looks impressive. It is, in Graeber's precise terminology, bullshit.

**We'd rather be busy than effective.** Gian Pierre's entire development process could fit in a tweet: "Described what I needed. AI built it. Shipped." That's terrifying. Where's the struggle? Where's the craft? Where are the 14-hour days and the war stories? We have built an entire professional identity around suffering through complexity. A chemist who skips all of it and ships better software in an evening is not inspiring — he's insulting.

**We protect what we know, not what works.** The React developer who spent three years mastering hooks, context, and state management has a legitimate emotional investment in that knowledge. Telling them a chemist just did their job in an evening with AI is like telling a calligrapher that the printing press is here. Technically a promotion for humanity. Personally devastating.

**We build empires, not solutions.** Graeber noticed that managers are evaluated by headcount. A director managing 20 engineers outranks one managing 5. This creates a gravitational pull toward hiring, toward complexity, toward inventing problems that justify the team's existence. Nobody gets promoted for saying "actually, one person with Claude Code could do this."

Sandi Metz nailed it in 2016: *"Duplication is far cheaper than the wrong abstraction."* The software industry heard that, nodded sagely, and immediately built seventeen more abstractions. Because abstractions create jobs. And jobs create org charts. And org charts create directors. And directors create the meetings that the flunkies attend.

**The sunk cost of identity.** This is the deepest one. We don't just learn frameworks — we *become* them. "I'm a React developer." "I'm a backend engineer." "I'm a DevOps specialist." These aren't job descriptions. They're identities. And identities don't go quietly. A chemist who builds production software without those identities isn't just a productivity story — he's an existential threat to every person whose sense of self is wrapped up in knowing how `useEffect` cleanup functions work.

## The 64x Multiplier

Gian Pierre produced roughly £90,000 of software in 70 hours. A junior developer at £20/hour produces roughly £20/hour of value. That's a **64x multiplier**.

Not because Gian Pierre is 64 times smarter. Because he carried zero overhead. No framework religion. No architecture astronautics. No ceremony. No translation layers. No identity crisis about whether he's a "real developer."

He's a chemist who needed a coaching platform. He described what it should do. The AI built it. He shipped it.

The 10x developer was always a myth. The real multiplier was never about typing speed or algorithm knowledge. It was about how much bullshit you could avoid. Gian Pierre avoided all of it — not by discipline, but by ignorance. He didn't know the bullshit existed, so he didn't do it.

That's not a bug. That's the most devastating feature request the software industry has ever received.

## Who Is the Genius Here?

I want to sit with this question honestly, because it's not rhetorical.

I've been building software professionally for years. I was busy refining my method, writing blog posts about it, giving it a name. Gian Pierre read one of those posts and used it better than I had. He took the MCP-first idea and applied it with a clarity that I — too inside my own head — couldn't manage.

He didn't have the context that would have stopped him. He didn't know which parts were supposed to be hard. He didn't have professional habits telling him to add another layer of abstraction. He just read what worked and did it.

Hardware access changed his trajectory. The MacBook I handed him that weekend wasn't just a faster machine — it was the difference between being able to run Claude Code and not. Between being able to build at all and being stuck.

But that's not the whole story either. The tools were there. The methodology was written down. He absorbed it and executed it better than most engineers I've worked with.

So who's the genius? I genuinely don't know. Maybe the question is wrong. Maybe "genius" is the wrong frame entirely when the constraint was never intelligence — it was access, and friction, and identity, and all the things we accidentally build around ourselves to protect our sense of what a "real developer" looks like.

## So What Should He Earn?

Three answers, depending on your worldview:

**The capitalist answer:** He produces £1,286/hour of value. Pay him accordingly. A company hiring Gian Pierre at £150K/year while he replaces a £400K team is getting the deal of a lifetime.

**The Graeber answer:** He should earn what the productive fraction of a traditional team earns — roughly £40K per project — but since he can do 10+ projects a year, that's £400K+. The difference between that and the team's total cost is the bullshit tax the industry has been charging.

**The honest answer:** It doesn't matter what we think he should earn. The market will decide. And the market is about to discover that domain experts with AI produce better software faster than professional developers drowning in ceremony. The pricing will adjust. It always does. Just ask the calligraphers.

If spike.land reaches its goal by March 21st, we might hire him. Not as charity. Because he's good. Because he figured something out that most engineers are still arguing about in Slack.
